# GA_-_PSO-
An analysis of the influence of Adversarial Machine Learning black-box algorithms on Network Intrusion Detection System datasets.

Over the past decade, the frequency and complexity of cyberattacks have increased significantly, posing substantial risks to corporations, governments, and individuals alike. According to the 2023 IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report, the global average cost of a data breach has surged to $4.45 million, reflecting a 15% rise over just three years [1]. Notably, financial institutions have been particularly vulnerable, as the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) estimates an alarming 200% increase in malware families targeting this sector between 2023 and 2024 [2]. In response to these urgent challenges, a Nasdaq study conducted in 2025 revealed that 61% of cybersecurity professionals intend to leverage artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) technologies to enhance their security systems [3].
The Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS) is widely recognized as a fundamental component of cybersecurity defense strategies. These systems are designed to monitor network traffic and identify potential threats, primarily employing signature-based techniques to detect established attack patterns. However, it is essential to note that signature-based systems often struggle to identify zero-day attacks and may lack adaptability in dynamic threat environments. Consequently, there has been a growing trend towards incorporating machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) methodologies into NIDS. These advanced techniques facilitate real-time anomaly detection and enhance overall threat response capabilities [4]. Despite their excellent accuracy, these models are susceptible to adversarial samples, intentionally generated inputs designed to deceive AI systems. AS studies show, such adversarial attacks can significantly degrade detection performance, especially in multiclass intrusion Scenarios [5].
Several solutions have been proposed to mitigate hostile threats, including adversarial training, ensemble learning, feature selection methodologies, and explainable AI models [4]. However, a significant concern remains.
Many existing adversarial defense models have been evaluated using outdated benchmark datasets, rather than more contemporary and realistic datasets such as CIC-IoT2023 and CSE CIC-IDS2018.
These evaluations often overlook the impact of modern adversarial techniques, particularly Genetic Algorithms (GA) and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), on the resilience of classifiers.
This study aims to fill the gap by evaluating the performance and susceptibility of classifiers, such as Random Forest and Decision Tree, under adversarial settings using GA and PSO. The emphasis is on determining which features are most affected, how dataset parameters influence attack success, and how new attacks undermine model robustness on an updated dataset like CIC-IoT2023.
The objective is to deliver actionable insights to develop resilient, explainable Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS) frameworks that can be assessed against contemporary attack patterns. Within this framework, the system will fulfill the following requirements:
Realistic Adversarial Attack Simulation: Utilize Genetic Algorithms (GA) and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) to develop adversarial real-world attack patterns. These attacks aim to evade detection by implementing minimal yet significant modifications to input attributes, while maintaining the integrity of network traffic formats.
i.Preserve provenance: flow_id, IP addresses, and timestamps remain unchanged, allowing each adversarial sample to be linked back to its source record. [6]
ii.Per-flow sparsity & global budget: Modifications are restricted to a maximum of three features per flow (recommended ≤ 3 features), and a budget for the entire dataset is implemented to ensure that attacks are discreet and limited in resources. 
iii.Allowed tweaks only: Only realistic flow statistics may be changed (e.g., small timing or packet-size nudges); IP/port and protocol-state fields are not altered. [4]
iv.Bounded, realistic changes: Every modified feature has a limit (recommended at ±15–20% or around =2σ of the harmless average) and is restricted to the minimum and maximum values of the observed dataset to ensure that the values remain realistic. [5]
v.Plausibility & hard checks: Applicants that breach fundamental invariants (nonnegative counts; fwd + bwd = total; bytes = pkts x avg_pkt_size; timing consistency) are either disqualified or significantly penalized. [7]
vi.Stealth-aware optimization: The fitness function gives more points for higher bengin scores and takes away points for the number, size, and uniqueness of changes. This means that solutions will prefer minor, standard, and low-cost adjustments. [5]
vii.Black-box transfer & query limits: Attacks are produced in a black-box environment that employs a transfer method, and where relevant, the query budgets or query efficiency (number of queries required for a successful evasion) are indicated. [8]
viii.Clear reporting & reproducibility: Experiments run with≥5 seeds and saved configurations; published outputs include evasion rate, mean cost edits, stealth distribution, queries per success, top changed features, and a CSV of original versus adversarial rows.
Testing with NIDS Classifiers: Adversarial samples should be tested using Random Forest.
Dataset Compatibility: The system must be capable of supporting and effectively pre-processing the CIC-IoT2023 datasets, ensuring that they are correctly formatted and optimized for thorough experimentation.
Evaluation of Classifier Robustness: The system must evaluate how adversarial samples impact the performance measure of classification.
